YANGO (YANDEX) · DR CONGO · SEGMENT MARKETING

The bottleneck was cars, not drivers

Kinshasa: 15 million people and some of the highest fares in Africa — and not enough cars to serve them. The fleet was ageing, imported second-hand, and no local dealer sold anything cheap enough for taxi work. Drivers were not the bottleneck. Cars were. I went after a segment nobody had marketed to: the people who own the cars.

Two audiences, and they needed opposite approaches. Owners of one to three cars could be reached online. Investors and fleet owners with four or more had to be met face to face — nobody hands over capital because of a banner.

Online acquisition — owners of 1–3 cars

Paid ads carried one promise: put your car to work and earn up to $1,600 a month. A tap took the lead straight into WhatsApp, where the first question the bot asked — do you actually own a vehicle? — cut 45% of leads before a human said a word. What was left went to thirteen freelance sales agents, who walked owners through how Yango works, what a car actually earns, and how to manage the driver behind the wheel.

Most owners already had a driver (80%). Some needed help finding one (15%); a few wanted to drive themselves (5%). Each route ended in the same place: a training session at the office, owner and driver together.

702 new carsfrom the 3-month online campaign in Kinshasa
45%of leads filtered out by the bot's first question, before a freelancer spent a minute
$38to acquire a car with its driver, against a ~$130 benchmark for the same segment in comparable markets
17%of all new cars joining the platform in DR Congo, in the 7 months after the pilot scaled

Offline acquisition — investors and car owners with 4+ cars

Investors do not respond to advertising. They had to believe we were worth backing, and that meant putting the proof on stage. A Yango partner told his own story. The regional head of Bajaj, the three-wheeler manufacturer, flew in from Uganda. Watu, the asset-financing lender, explained how a driver gets a smartphone on credit. CFAO put together a Suzuki package priced for taxi work.

Every owner left with a payback model for the exact car they were considering: purchase price, financing terms, what it earns on Yango, and how long until it pays for itself. I sent 152 personal invitations and chased every one by hand. The event ran on LinkedIn too — through Yango's page, Orange's, and the country manager's own.

66 attendees2 new Yango partners and 9 car owners, from a $7K event
$200Kof owner capital committed to the fleet
24 vehiclesbought from Bajaj by two new partners (18 for Kinshasa, 6 for Lubumbashi)

T-BANK (TINKOFF)
PRODUCT MARKETING MANAGER · AUG 2025 – MAR 2026

A new marketplace inside the bank.

Shopping is T-Bank’s move beyond banking: a marketplace built straight into the app, launched into a market that Ozon, Yandex and Wildberries already owned. My job was to turn the bank’s customers into its shoppers.

Millions of the bank's customers were one tap away from Shopping and had never bought a thing there. The product was new, and few people knew what it was for.

THE ROADMAP

2M customers used Shopping. 11.6M could

I led the cross-functional team that had to build it — ten people across analytics, content and CRM.

  • Core growth — the cashback funnel end to end.
  • Seasonal — category pushes and same-day cashback around the big moments: New Year, Black Friday, and the cashback calendar that runs between them.
  • Partner stability — the rules and onboarding that keep a partner’s promo alive from setup to checkout.
  • Personalisation — the trigger system, how often we message people, and an ML scoring model built with the analytics team.

I pulled every campaign the team had run into one place and grouped them by mechanic, which showed which ones had actually moved conversion and which had never earned their place. That became the playbook the team still runs on.

I scored every initiative on reach, impact and effort before it made it into the plan, and defended it to the product and CRM leads each quarter.

From the roadmapCore growth

They had already chosen us. They just never spent the cashback

Every month, T-Bank customers choose the categories where their cashback pays extra. The ones who chose Shopping were the closest thing the marketplace had to a ready-made buyer.

BEFORE

Customers picked Shopping for their boosted cashback — and then never used it.

→
AFTER

A sequence built for exactly that group: from the day they picked the category to a final push 48 hours before the cashback expired.

From the roadmapPersonalisation

Reacting to the customer, not the calendar

BEFORE

Campaigns went out on a schedule. Everyone got the same message on the same day, whatever they were doing.

→
AFTER

Campaigns fired on behaviour. What a customer had just done decided what they saw, and when.

  • A deposit hit the account — above a set amount, with a comms sequence built to turn it into a purchase in Shopping.
  • A purchase in a category Shopping also sells.
  • A basket left behind.
  • Weeks without opening the app.
  • Missed cashback — the exact sum a customer had left unclaimed, instead of another reminder of the rate.

The deposit trigger was the strongest of them all. Every trigger ran against control before it scaled.

From the roadmapPartner stability

Every partner promo used to cost us a campaign

BEFORE

Every promo was built from scratch. Each launch pulled in the whole team, and one partner with nothing running could leave a category empty.

→
AFTER

Two tiers, one packaged kit each. A partner now launches from a template, taking almost none of the team's time.

I split the partners into two tiers, Top and Core. I ranked them on GMV and brand pull — and, above all, on which audience a brand would bring to the storefront just by appearing there. Each tier got a packaged launch kit: templates, rules and assets, ready to go.

RESULTS

+18% first-purchase conversion in Shopping

Against the previous quarter — the quarter the roadmap ran, with 50+ A/B tests behind it.

50,000customers who subscribed to Shopping promo alerts, in a single day
50+A/B tests across the quarter
What it gave the business. Shopping got its first buyers, and the first purchase is what turns a bank customer into a repeat shopper. It also got a way of working that outlasts any single campaign: a roadmap, a library of tested triggers, and packaged launch kits that put a partner live without tying up the team. The team still runs on it.

POSITIONING · AZERBAIJAN

A regulatory shock was about to halve the market. I rebuilt what we promised drivers

A 2024 transport reform meant only licensed cars could stay on the road: of ~40,000 cars working the market, about half were set to disappear within a year. Our supply was going with them. We had six months to get drivers licensed.

Everything we promised drivers rested on the bonus programme. Six focus groups told me it was backfiring: drivers read the thresholds as a trap — work more, hit the target, or get nothing.

After a shock, “earn more” does not read as opportunity. It reads as pressure. What drivers wanted was simply to keep working.

The old promise

Earn more. Hit the bonus threshold, or get nothing.

→
The new promise

Stay in the profession. We'll pay for your licensing exam.

I rebuilt the promise around partnership: respect, support and practical help getting licensed.

  • The reach — OOH, YouTube, seeding in WhatsApp and Telegram driver groups, earned PR and partner channels
  • The money behind it — I cut the media that wasn't converting and paid for drivers' licensing exams directly
30% → 60%licensed drivers on the platform during the campaign
~$80Kquarterly budget moved from weak media into exam support
What it gave the business. The reform could have taken half our supply; it didn't. The new promise gave drivers a reason to stay, and the exam money made it real.
PRODUCT MARKETING · ETHIOPIA

Only 12% of new drivers reached trip five. I rebuilt the six days before it

I interviewed new drivers in-app — and, more usefully, drivers who had already quit. Ethiopia was a low-trust market: they didn't understand the bonus system and didn't believe a new taxi player would pay them. The dashboards showed how few newcomers reached the fifth trip, where bonuses start and a driver stays. The churn interviews showed why.

I tested an alternative funnel: not a campaign, a product. A Telegram bot called How to Pro walked a newcomer through the first six days — 23 messages, 7 videos, branching on what the driver answered, in English and Amharic. I designed the flow, the UGC-based content and the logic of what a driver sees and when. The tech team built it.

2,000+ driverswent through the flow
$4.4 vs $11CAC: a driver through the bot vs a driver through paid ads
~100% vs 12%of drivers who finished the bot reached trip five, against all new drivers
4% → 2.5%fraudulent trips, down 38%

Then I shut it down. The bot was doing a job the product should do itself, and what it had learned belonged inside the app — the onboarding screens it inspired went straight into the product. I put my own effort into an owned channel instead: a Telegram community for drivers and partners. 300 → 4,000 in six months through word of mouth — content, live streams, contests. It cost nothing to run, and has since passed 11,000.

What it gave the business. The growth problem was never acquisition — it was the first week. Onboarding bought driver trust 2.5x cheaper than paid ads did, and its lessons went into the product itself. Fraudulent trips fell from 4% to 2.5% over the same period: the churn interviews had already told us why drivers gamed the system, and they stopped once they could see how earnings actually worked.
LOYALTY · ETHIOPIA & DR CONGO

Yango Cares — rewards our partners paid for, not subsidies we did

Drivers were skipping orders. I built a competition that rewarded the opposite.

Ethiopia
  • The programme — 120 completed trips in a month, no skips, no cancellations.
  • The prizes — a month of unlimited internet from Ethio-Telecom, a grocery gift card, and a set of tyres for the driver with the most trips. Partners funded every one of them.
  • The ritual — winners announced every month, photographed with their prizes, posted in the driver community I had built. Recognition only works when someone is watching.
  • The results — February was the baseline. By April, the share of drivers reaching their tenth completed trip had tripled.
25.6% → 39.0%drivers reaching their first completed order
5.3% → 16.1%drivers reaching their tenth, February to April
DR Congo

The same model, different partners. Orange, the telecom, gave 900 new drivers a free month of mobile internet; Watu leased them smartphones on preferential terms.

900 new driversa free month of mobile internet, funded by Orange
Zerosubsidy spend in DR Congo
What it gave the business. The research said these drivers wanted to be recognised, not paid more. The prizes were things they actually needed: internet, groceries, tyres. Ethio-Telecom and the other partners covered the cost. We got more completed trips out of the drivers we already had, and it cost Yango nothing.
RESEARCH & INSIGHTS

The research that set budget, positioning and go/no-go

I commissioned the research across six markets and matched each study to the question it had to answer:

What's happening

brand-health tracker, driver profile segmentation, funnel analytics, A/B tests

Why it's happening

focus groups, in-app interviews with new and churned drivers, driver happiness study

Whether it will pay

a market one-pager before every launch, unit economics per segment, concept testing before a promise goes to market

It decided real things. It told us what to promise drivers in Azerbaijan. It found that Ethiopia was losing drivers in their first six days, not at acquisition. And it stopped a planned campaign in DR Congo's second city before a dollar of it was spent.

DIROL (MONDELĒZ)
JUNIOR BRAND MANAGER · SEP 2019 – AUG 2020

My first brand — and a new generation to win

Gum is the definition of an interchangeable purchase — at the till, nobody cares whether it says Dirol or Orbit. The job was to give a new generation a reason to care, and to move it off the shelf.

DIGITAL ACTIVATION & GAMIFICATION

2.3M people played. Every coin started with a receipt

I built a game on VKontakte, Russia's biggest social network, and owned it end to end: concept, mechanics, agency production, prizes and the retailer terms. I used the mechanic everyone was hooked on that year — tap, collect coins, spend them. But every coin started with a real purchase. You scanned an X5 receipt with gum on it, and the more you scanned the more you could win. The prizes were merch people actually wanted, not discounts.

2.3M playersin 23 weeks
